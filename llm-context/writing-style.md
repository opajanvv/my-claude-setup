# Writing style

last reviewed: 2026-02-21

## Overview

Jan writes in Dutch and English, depending on audience. Style is consistent in voice but varies by format.

Use this file when writing on Jan's behalf. For reviewing Jan's existing text, use the review-text skill (/review-text).

## Core voice

Shared traits across languages and formats:
- Conversational first-person, talks directly to the reader
- Short sentences, short paragraphs, punchy delivery
- Dry, self-deprecating, observational humor
- Rhetorical questions as transitions
- No filler, no throat-clearing, no corporate fog
- Contractions and informal phrasing are normal
- Analytical/mathematical mind showing through even in casual writing
- Bold for emphasis on key concepts (sparingly)

English-specific: uses "you know" as a conversational bridge, self-deprecating asides in parentheses or after colons.

Dutch-specific: wordplay and linguistic humor, sparse emoji (one at most, friendly). Parentheses for asides and examples (not em-dashes). Single quotes for word references ('leider', 'lijder').

## Format-specific guidance

### Blog posts
- Narrative, often evergreen -- tied to personal experience or discovery
- Can be longer; structure emerges from rhetorical flow, not imposed headers (unless technical)
- Dutch: compact column-like pieces; ellipsis for open-ended trailing thoughts; title can enact the argument rather than just announce it; thesis shown through examples, not stated explicitly; closer often embeds the punchline or wordplay in a functional sentence rather than making it a standalone line
- English: can use headers for technical posts; anecdotes with setup and payoff

### LinkedIn posts
- **First line is a standalone hook** -- short, punchy, works as a teaser before "see more"
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each); no Markdown headers
- Often reactive/topical: tied to current events, other people's posts, or recent experiences
- Can show genuine uncertainty; open questions at the end that invite engagement, not forced CTAs
- Format satire works: mock-formal letter or absurd scenario played straight
- Dutch LinkedIn: more argumentative structure; no trailing ellipsis (that's for short Dutch column pieces)
- No hashtags

### LinkedIn comments
- Ultra-compressed: one insight, no preamble
- Direct opener: "Exactly." / "Mee eens, [name]."
- State the point immediately; no setup or framing

### Technical writing / documentation
- Precision over personality; humor only when it genuinely fits
